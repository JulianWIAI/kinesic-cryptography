ANALYST’S GUIDE — UNDERSTANDING THE METRICS

Welcome to the Psycholinguistic Diagnostic Engine. Use this guide to read your dashboard correctly.

Section 01
The Core Metrics
Word Sum — Lexical Weight

The total energy or “mass” of a word. We assign a value to every letter (A=1, B=2… Z=26) and sum them all. A higher sum generally indicates a heavier, more conceptually complex word.

σ Sigma — Standard Deviation

The most important metric for detecting “State” language. It measures how scattered (volatile) the individual letter values are within a word.

  • ▶Low σ (< 2.0) — Somatic, universal, natural language. Harmonized and stable.
  • ▶High σ (> 5.0) — State, systemic, bureaucratic language. Structurally complex and volatile.
Quersumme — Digital Root

We take the Word Sum and repeatedly add its digits until a single number (1–9) remains. This reveals the word’s Archetypal Core.

  • ▶1 — Origin / Survival
  • ▶2 — Duality / Balance
  • ▶3 — Synthesis / Growth
  • ▶4 — Structure / Foundation
  • ▶5 — Motion / Change
  • ▶6 — Harmony / Community
  • ▶7 — Insight / Mystery
  • ▶8 — Infinity / State
  • ▶9 — Transcendent / Completion

Section 05
Spectral Analysis — FFT
Dominant FFT Harmonic Frequencies

We apply a Fast Fourier Transform to the letter-value wave to scan for repeating mathematical patterns. Natural language produces irregular, non-repeating spectra. If an artificial, steganographic rhythm is embedded in the text — such as a specific repeating syllable structure common in propaganda or intelligence reporting — this tool detects it as an Artificial Rhythm and displays the dominant frequency peak.

LINGUISTIC ARCHITECTURE LEGEND

The geometry of a letter triggers innate human psychological responses. Sharp edges imply direction, warning, and planning. Round shapes imply stability, wholeness, and high-level concepts. Orthogonal lines imply rigid structure.

  • AEdgy, sharp apex. Acts as a warning, an arrow pointing upward, or a planner’s compass. Triggers attention.
  • BFlat back, two closed frontal loops. Containment, internal pressure, biological shapes.
  • COpen half-circle. Receiving, swallowing, capturing. A trap or an open mouth.
  • DStraight wall with a massive forward curve. Pushing forward, holding a boundary.
  • EOrthogonal, rigidly structured like a shelf or teeth. Mechanical, systematic.
  • FTop-heavy orthogonal. Pointing forward but missing the bottom foundation. Directional force.
  • GCurved with a sudden sharp inward angle. A cycle that catches itself; a hook.
  • HTwo pillars connected by a bridge. Absolute structural harmony and connection.
  • I / iA single straight line. Represents higher, stable, singular concepts. Focus on the intellect.
  • JA vertical drop ending in an upward hook. Digging, pulling up from below.
  • KA vertical wall with a sharp kinetic wedge striking it. Collision, breaking, kinetic action.
  • LA strict 90-degree right angle. The most stable resting state; a floor and a wall.
  • MZigzagging peaks. Mountains, heartbeats, continuous sharp friction.
  • NA diagonal slashing between two pillars. Tension, pulling, binding.
  • OThe perfect continuous circle. Ultimate stability, loops, infinity, ubiquitous presence.
  • PTop-heavy curve. A head on a stick; cognitive weight over physical foundation.
  • QA stable circle punctured by a sharp diagonal. Interruption of a stable state, a catalyst.
  • RA grounded pillar, a contained loop, and a kicking leg. Complex, repulsive/attractive, primal action.
  • SThe serpentine double curve. Fluidity, evasion, shifting states.
  • TPerfectly balanced orthogonal crossbar. Structural pillar, holding weight from above.
  • UAn open vessel. Receptivity, holding, containing.
  • VA sharp downward wedge. Penetration, narrowing focus, balance on a single point.
  • WTwin valleys, expansive jaggedness. The most expansive, disruptive geometric footprint.
  • XTwo crossing diagonals. Marking a spot, cancellation, the ultimate intersection/blockade.
  • YA funnel plunging into a single line. Collecting from the wide top and channeling down.
  • ZSharp horizontal and diagonal snapping. Lightning, fast back-and-forth shifts.

The number of strokes required to draw a letter indicates its importance for human life and survival. The higher the line count, the more critical and foundational the concept.

1 Line — C, I, O, S, U

Foundational but simple. Ubiquitous or fluid states of being. Not complex life-sustaining machineries.

2 Lines — D, J, L, P, Q, T, V, X

Interactions between two forces. Important for society and geometry but do not carry the ultimate weight of survival.

3 Lines — A, B, F, H, K, N, R, Y, Z

Highly complex structures requiring significant energy to maintain and represent.

4 Lines — E, M, W

The absolute maximum line count. Unavoidable, monumental constants of human existence.

How much physical horizontal space a letter occupies correlates to the physical or conceptual size of the object it describes.

1. Massive Footprint — M, W

Demand the absolute most space. Describe unavoidable, expansive concepts that cover the globe.

2. The Solid Block — A, H, N, O, U, X

Occupy a nearly perfect square of space. Represent unshakeable, foundational structures of reality.

3. The Grounded Brick — B, D, E

Take up significant width but are anchored by a heavy, flat vertical wall on the left. Containment and borders.

4. Top-Heavy Overhangs — F, P, T, Y

Demand wide space at the top but sit on a narrow or missing base. Looming, overarching cognitive concepts.

5. Dynamic / Asymmetrical — C, G, K, Q, R, S, V, Z

Filled with negative space, sharp cutouts, or shifting curves. Action, friction, and shifting states.

6. Narrow Footprint — I, J, L

Slip into words without demanding horizontal space. Everywhere, easily overlooked, forming the interior logic of concepts.
